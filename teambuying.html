Project overview

The product

Guided Buying(GB) guides employees through buying process

Guided Buying(GB) is a professional procurement platform for employees to buy materials and services, and provides guidance on policies and compliance through the experience.

GB's users includes both professional and casual buyers. Professional buyers buy frequently for the company. Causal buyers buy occasionally.

The feature

Contribute to the same requisitions with your team

Team Buying is a collaborative feature that allows multiple users to work on the same requisition. Now, buyers can create a team of experts in different fields and assign roles to each team member, and everyone will have access and pool their knowledge to the same requisitions.

As the sole designer, I designed the end-to-end experience from creation and management, to buying and receiving. I collaborated with the researcher to define questions and analyze findings, with PMs on project scope and requirements, with visual designer to create new components, and with engineers to tackle technical challenges.

The problem

How might we have multiple people to access the same requisitions?

Many clients complained that their employees have to work in shifts because they cannot collaborate on the same documents. And they have to hire a central service team to take over requisitions when an employee is away or leaves the job. Also, there is a lack of transparency.

Every employee can only own one team and shop with this team, restricted by GB's dependence on its engine - P2P(Procure-to-Pay)

Initial design

User stories

A simple story - team owner creates a requisition, and team members join & edit the requisition

The original project scope was to only focus on the buying and receiving experience. We collected the user needs from the customer workshops and surveys and created the following four user stories around the main stages.

"As a team owner, I'm able to start a requisition with my team from the beginning"

"As a team owner, I am able to change the settings in a later process, as the checkout."

"As a team member, I should be notified when the team I'm part of starts a new requisition"

"As a team member, I should be able to join the team requisitions and edit it."

New scope

Turn constraints into opportunities

While the initial design was going on, we sent out surveys to customers and did the competitive analysis. According to the results, we decided to give the project more time, expand the scope, and flesh out the full feature to be more powerful.

1

Add team management into the scope.
  • Due to the dependency on P2P, each user could only own one team. Localize the team management would empower users to have more control and flexibility.

2

Expand user roles, adding admin & viewer.
  • According to the survey, 69% of users think teams should be predefined by the company. And 71% of users saw this feature as a means of adding transparency.

3

Create new components in Fiori Styleguide
  • SAP Fiori team is revamping the whole style guide. It's an excellent opportunity for me to collaborate with them and bring more modern components into the system.

Iteration 1.

Start shopping with a team in 2 steps

57% of customers would like to turn on team buying from the start.

Initial design

✘ There were 4 steps with a lot of irrelevant information to start the process.

  1. Start “On behalf of” on homepage;
  2. Choose to shop on behalf of “Team”;
  3. Search for a team;
  4. Create an empty requisition and confirm other details;
  5. Start shopping with the selected team.

New design

✔ Simplify the process in just 2 steps.

  1. Start “Buy with a team” on homepage;
  2. Choose a “Team”;
  3. Start shopping with the selected team.

Iteration 2.

Change the settings on the checkout page

60% would change the settings(turn on/off team buying, change teams) in the later process.

Initial design


✘ Toggle button plays both roles of action and visual cue, which doesn't meet users' mental model;


✘ There was no friction when users turn off Team Buying.

New design


✔ Separate the action and visual cue;


✔ Use more obvious industry standardized circles as teams' visual cue;


✔ Add friction to the settings' changes by hiding the action in "..."

Iteration 3.

Admin manages teams in a functional and modern looking table

90% of users need to buy with teams that are predefined by the company.

Initial design

✘ The open drawer made team members hidden and wasted a lot of space;
✘ All action items are hidden in dropdowns.

Final design

✔ Support bulk actions;
✔ Use the side panel to show more team information without losing context;
✔ Lay out actions and place them to the directly associated content.

Iteration 4.

Admin creates teams in 2 methods

The primary method for the admin to configure GB is through uploading CSV files, and we also want to provide the flexibility to edit through the UI.

Initial design


✘ Loss of context when creating due to the old admin framework;


✘ Mixed uploading CSV and creating through UI in one process;


✘ All edits happen in one dialog, which is not flexible or reusable for different roles.

Final design

✔ Move all the actions on the team management page;
✔ Admin can create a team either by uploading CSV file or through UI;
✔ The creation process is broken down into 2 steps, which gives more flexibility for separative edits and could be reusable for end-user management based on roles.

Iteration 5.

End-users manage their teams in a hierarchical structure.

Most users wouldn't own more than 10 teams but can belong to as many as 30 teams.

Initial design


✘ The current GB list layout looked very busy;


✘ All the teams(users own and belong to) are mixed with no hierarchy.

Final design


✔ Use card components and add white space to create an airy feeling, and keep consistent across buying & admin experience;


✔ Use carousel for teams users own and table for teams they participate. This not only makes sense based on different numbers of team allocation but also creates a visual hierarchy.

Iteration 6.

End-user creates teams in 2 steps

Break down the creation process into tasks in each step makes the management flexible and possible for different roles.

Initial design


✘ Team information and team members assignments all in one modal;


✘ Contributors can not reuse the same modal since they could only add and manage team members but not the team information.

Final design


✔ Enter team information in the first step, and select team members and assign roles in the second step;


✔ During the management, users could only choose to edit specific fields;


✔ No need to redesign a different modal for the contributor who can only add and manage team members.

Iteration 7.

Automatic team buying for professional buyers and default team concept

It's a special use case from some clients where their employees purchase with teams 95% of the time.

Initial design

✘ Introduce the automatic TB in the first-day-experience modal, which was very interruptive and made other clients who have no such use case confused;
✘ The edit was in a modal and hidden in the gear icon, which added an extra click.

Reflection

The impact

This feature introduced the first "team" feature into GB, which reduces the redundancies and workarounds, and boosts efficiency and adds transparency to the requisition process. For the feature itself, Team Buying reduces the cost of customers hiring experts to only focus on requisitions for the teams and makes it available to any employees with certain access. At the same time, the feature can rollout to be a future team concept framework, being built into chat, procurement workspace, on behalf of, delegation, etc. And this is only the first step.

The challenge

Firstly, understand the pain points without direct access to end-users is always the hardest for 2B products. Secondly, technical dependencies are a unique challenge for GB. The limitations of P2P and Sourcing engines restrict what GB could achieve and build. However, we maximized and grew this feature with the best we could as a team. We hosted CEI workshops, conducted surveys, etc., trying to understand the real problems. And at the same time, we made many innovative changes that could potentially influence the future roadmap.
